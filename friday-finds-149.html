How I Did It — The Prompts

Step 1 — Discover Sources Prompts

I used two separate prompts to surface research for the debate. NotebookLM's Discover Sources finds web content — these queries targeted the most recent data (2025–2026 only).


Prompt 1 — Environmental impact of AI:

  • Energy consumption and carbon footprint of large language models and generative AI systems — data centers, water usage, and environmental cost comparisons
  • How does individual or organizational AI use contribute to overall AI environmental impact — usage statistics and infrastructure analysis
  • Policy and regulatory approaches to reducing AI environmental impact — government intervention, corporate accountability, and infrastructure-level solutions
  • Individual carbon footprint as corporate responsibility-shifting strategy — history and parallels to AI energy debate
  • Role of organizational behavior and consumer choices in driving sustainable technology adoption — signaling, norm-setting, and market pressure
  • Nonprofit organizations and responsible AI adoption — ethical frameworks, environmental considerations, and mission alignment

Prompt 2 — Nonprofit advocacy effectiveness:

  • How nonprofit organizations and civic groups have successfully influenced technology policy and corporate behavior through advocacy campaigns
  • Evidence that organized public pressure and coalition building changes tech industry environmental practices
  • Nonprofit advocacy effectiveness — strategies for moving government policy on emerging technology regulation
  • How civil society organizations have shaped AI governance and regulation through public education and political engagement

Step 2 — The Debate Prompt

Once my sources were loaded, I used this prompt in the NotebookLM Audio Overview chat to generate the debate format.


Create an Oxford-style debate audio overview for an audience of nonprofit leaders and mission-driven professionals.


Motion: "This house believes that individual AI restraint is a distraction from the real environmental fight."


Format: Oxford debate with two speakers — one proposing the motion (FOR) and one opposing it (AGAINST). A neutral moderator introduces the debate, manages transitions, and closes the session. Speakers should actively disagree with each other and directly rebut specific claims made by the opposing side.


Structure:

  • Moderator introduces the motion and its relevance to nonprofit leaders
  • Proposition opening argument (FOR the motion)
  • Opposition opening argument (AGAINST the motion)
  • Proposition rebuttal
  • Opposition rebuttal
  • Proposition closing summary — strongest points only
  • Opposition closing summary — strongest points only
  • Moderator closes with no declared winner, framing the tension as an open question for the audience to sit with

Evidence: Both speakers should draw on the source material provided, including data on AI energy consumption, research on individual versus systemic environmental impact, and evidence on nonprofit and civic advocacy effectiveness. Arguments should be specific and evidence-grounded, not abstract.


Tone: Intellectually serious but accessible. Passionate but not polemical. Appropriate for senior nonprofit professionals who are thoughtful about both AI adoption and environmental responsibility. This is a genuine debate, not a panel discussion — speakers should actively disagree, directly challenge each other's claims, and push back with specific evidence rather than talking past each other.


Key arguments to ensure are represented:

  • FOR the motion: Individual and organizational AI footprints are negligible compared to infrastructure-level consumption; restraint culture can defund beneficial use cases; the real leverage is policy advocacy and public education
  • AGAINST the motion: Collective individual behavior signals values and builds political credibility; organizational restraint can model and influence sector-wide norms; "wait for systemic change" has historically been used to defer personal responsibility indefinitely

Do not declare a winner. End with both sides owning their strongest argument.

Introduction

Speaker 1 Welcome to the debate. Imagine standing at the base of a massive hydroelectric dam. You're watching millions of gallons of water just roaring through these giant industrial turbines. It is generating the power for entire cities.
Speaker 2 It's deafening, honestly. It's massive. And then someone walks up to you, hands you a tiny plastic thimble, and says, "Here, start bailing. We really need to conserve the river."
Speaker 1 Yeah, it's a striking image. And I mean, it perfectly captures the tension we are grappling with today.
Speaker 2 Right, because that is exactly how it feels to navigate the current technological landscape, especially if you are leading a mission-driven organization. We are tackling a pretty profound paradox today in our Oxford-style format.

The Motion

FOR We are. The motion before us is this: This house believes that individual AI restraint is a distraction from the real environmental fight. And you know, this isn't just some theoretical exercise.
AGAINST No, not at all. By this year, 2026, artificial intelligence is deeply embedded in the nonprofit sector. I mean, these are organizations fundamentally driven by social and environmental values, but they are colliding with the staggering physical environmental costs of the data centers powering their tools.
FOR Exactly, and they have to make a choice about how they respond to that collision. They do. Do we maximize our mission impact using every digital tool available, or do we pull back out of a sense of environmental stewardship? I will be proposing the motion today. I'm arguing that focusing on individual or organizational AI prompts — obsessing over our micro-level digital footprints — well, it diverts crucial time and energy. It takes political capital away from what actually moves the needle.
AGAINST And you think that's systemic policy?
FOR Yes, systemic policy advocacy and infrastructure regulation. The environmental crisis driven by AI is a macro-level infrastructure failure. Treating it as a consumer usage problem is not just ineffective; it's, frankly, a dangerous distraction.
AGAINST Well, I will be opposing the motion. I will argue that collective organizational restraint is not a distraction at all; it is actually the absolute foundation for establishing sector-wide norms. You simply cannot separate external advocacy from internal operational integrity. Restraint is what maintains donor credibility, and crucially, it is the only thing that drives the aggregate market demand necessary to force the tech giants to actually develop sustainable tech.

The Mechanics of Physical Scale

FOR Okay, well let's get right into the mechanics of this, starting with my side of the motion. The core of my argument really rests on a simple mechanism of physical scale. The overwhelming size of AI infrastructure renders your individual restraint statistically meaningless. Let's look at the baseline data for 2026. Global data centers are projected to consume 1,050 terawatt-hours of electricity this year.
AGAINST Which is an almost unfathomable number until you ground it.
FOR Exactly. To put that in the physical world, 1,050 terawatt-hours rivals the electricity demand of the entire nation of Japan. We're watching facilities like Meta's Hyperion Center in Louisiana draw more than twice the power of the entire city of New Orleans. Because of that structural scale, the push for individual AI restraint is a misdirection. It's a sleight of hand. Think back to 2004. The fossil fuel giant BP essentially popularized the concept of the personal carbon footprint. They ran a massive advertising campaign to get everyday people to calculate their own footprint.
AGAINST Right, it was a brilliant PR move.
FOR Brilliant and incredibly cynical. It was a deliberate corporate strategy designed to shift the burden of responsibility away from industrial fossil fuel extractors and onto everyday consumers. They wanted you agonizing over whether you left the hallway light on rather than questioning the millions of barrels of oil they were pulling out of the ground. Because we are seeing the exact same playbook unfold right now with AI. The tech giants are perfectly happy to watch a nonprofit team agonize over whether to use ChatGPT to draft a grant proposal rather than having that same team question the fossil fuels powering the tech giant's grid. For a lean nonprofit, automating administrative tasks with AI saves an estimated 15 to 20 hours a week. Asking that overworked team to put down the tool does not close a coal plant in Virginia. It doesn't cool a server farm. All it does is starve that nonprofit of vital capacity to do their actual work.

Inference and "Shadow AI"

AGAINST I understand the appeal of that framing. It's comforting to say, "The problem is so big, my actions don't matter, so I might as well use the tools." But dismissing micro-level usage completely ignores the physical reality of how AI actually consumes power. Yes, training a massive foundational model is an incredibly energy-intensive one-time event. It's like building a massive factory. But the ongoing daily use of these models — the phase we call inference — is fundamentally different. When you type a prompt into a generative AI, the system doesn't just pull a pre-written answer out of a filing cabinet. It has to calculate the statistical probability for every single word it generates across billions of parameters in real time. That requires immense computational power. And right now, inference — that daily querying — accounts for 80 to 90% of total AI energy consumption.
FOR 80 to 90%?
AGAINST Yes. The factory's built, sure, but inference is the cost of keeping the machines running 24/7. It scales directly with our daily usage. But the ocean is made of drops. If we look at the 2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report, nearly half of nonprofits currently lack formal AI governance policies. Only 7% say AI is strategically embedded with clear guardrails. When you brush off organizational restraint as a distraction, you are giving the entire sector a free pass to embrace an unconstrained consumption model. Collective individual behavior is exactly what signals values to the market. Historically, waiting for systemic change has been the most convenient excuse to defer personal responsibility indefinitely. If we don't build accountability internally, how can we ever demand it externally?

Credibility and Donor Trust

FOR Wait, but if we look at how change actually happens, we have to talk about where our leverage lies. You're suggesting that market signaling through reduced usage is the lever. I argue the real lever comes from policy, not from abstaining from practice. Look at Greenpeace's historic "Click Clean" campaign. They didn't tell environmentalists to stop using the internet. They successfully forced tech giants to adopt renewable energy through aggressive public benchmarking, legislative pressure, and naming and shaming. Or look at the EU AI Act. Does a nonprofit abstaining from using a generative AI tool in their back office actually put an ounce of pressure on Microsoft or Google to clean up their grid? I argue it absolutely does not. The only pressure those corporations respond to is aggressive shareholder activism and state-level moratoriums. Look at New York Senate bill S9144, proposing a moratorium on new data center permits. That is the battlefield. If we waste our limited resources auditing our own staff's AI prompts, we take our eyes off the legislative battles that can actually mandate 100% carbon-free electricity.
AGAINST But you are creating a false dichotomy between internal practice and external policy advocacy. The two are inextricably linked because advocacy requires moral authority. A nonprofit cannot effectively lobby for stringent environmental regulations if its own house is a mess. It invalidates it through a complete loss of institutional credibility. Recent data shows that 47% of employees are engaging in Shadow AI — completely bypassing corporate guardrails. If almost half of a climate advocacy group's staff is secretly using highly energy-intensive AI without any ethical oversight to draft their climate newsletters, how can that organization sit across the table from a state legislator and demand accountability? It hollows out your leverage. You can't demand a city build a state-of-the-art recycling plant while you are actively dumping trash in your own lobby. And donors are watching. 43% of donors report that an organization's AI use affects their trust.

Mission Impact and the Jevons Paradox

FOR Okay, well you bring up funding and the core mission, which is actually where I think your argument runs into a serious practical wall. We shouldn't blunt our absolute best tools just because the tech industry hasn't cleaned up its grid yet. AI tools are currently driving 20 to 30% increases in donations for nonprofits through predictive analytics and personalized outreach. Preaching a culture of restraint risks creating a chilling effect that literally defunds the mission. If a nonprofit decides to limit its AI usage out of a sense of micro-level guilt and consequently loses that 30% fundraising boost, they have fewer resources to lobby for the New York moratoriums. Defunding your own mission to save a few watt-hours of electricity is a strategic failure.
AGAINST Defunding the mission is a real risk, but you are ignoring the unintended consequences of unchecked efficiency — the Jevons paradox. In 2025, Google managed to reduce the per-prompt energy of their Gemini model by 33 times. On paper, that sounds incredibly green. But because the tool is so easy and seemingly cheap to run, the volume of usage absolutely skyrockets. We aren't just doing what we did before more efficiently; we are generating millions of high-resolution images we don't need and summarizing endless mundane documents we could have just skimmed. That efficiency completely wipes out the environmental gains. And it gets worse when we look at water. A single hyper-scale data center can evaporate up to 5 million gallons of water daily. In Dallas, Oregon, Google's data centers consume more than a quarter of all the water used in the entire city.

Closing Arguments

FOR I hear your point on right-sizing, and technically I completely agree. But we must look at how we actually force the market to offer those smaller, cleaner models to the public. It does not happen through quiet individual abstinence; it happens through top-down regulatory frameworks. My argument distills down to identifying where our leverage actually exists in the fight against climate change. The environmental crisis driven by AI is fundamentally an infrastructure failure. Nonprofits must aggressively adopt and utilize AI to scale their impact and fight their respective fights without this paralyzing guilt.
AGAINST And for the opposition, my summary rests on the physical reality that accountability cannot be compartmentalized. Internal accountability is the prerequisite for external pressure. Restraint is not a distraction; it is the prerequisite for leadership. We cannot wait for systemic change while we are actively fueling the crisis.

Moderator Close

FOR Stepping back, the tension we've explored today highlights a profound friction for modern organizations. Despite our different approaches, our discussion today revealed a deeply concerning shared truth. The rapid adoption of AI is bringing staggering, often entirely hidden physical costs.
AGAINST As is the tradition of this show, we will not declare a winner today. We leave this debate unresolved, framing it as an open, urgent question for you, our listeners.
FOR Think back to that massive hydroelectric dam. The water is rushing. The question isn't whether you should use the electricity. The question is whether you are willing to fight for who controls the river while still taking responsibility for your thimble. Thank you for joining us on The Debate.
